Thriving Through Menopause: A Scientific and Strategic Guide

By Nicholas J. Sisco, Ph.D.

Menopause marks a significant biological transition—not a failure of the body, but a recalibration of its systems. It typically occurs between ages 45–55 and is defined by 12 consecutive months without menstruation, following a natural decline in ovarian hormone production. While the physiological changes can be profound, they are neither random nor unmanageable. With the right strategies, women can emerge from this phase stronger, clearer, and more metabolically resilient.

1. Macronutrient Strategy: Protein-Led Nutrition

Estrogen plays a protective role in maintaining muscle mass and bone density. As it declines, sarcopenia accelerates—unless counteracted. Women in midlife often under-consume protein, particularly leucine-rich sources critical for muscle protein synthesis.

  • Aim for 1.2–1.6 g protein/kg body weight/day, distributed across meals
  • Prioritize leucine-dense proteins, e.g., eggs, whey, poultry, legumes
  • Include glycine-rich foods, e.g., bone-in cuts, gelatin
  • Ensure adequate vitamin C to support endogenous collagen synthesis

Note: Collagen supplements are broken down like all proteins. What matters most is total protein intake, amino acid diversity, and training stimulus.

2. Resistance Training: Enhancing Neuroplasticity Through BDNF

Resistance training serves as a potent stimulus for maintaining muscle and bone health during menopause. Beyond these benefits, it plays a crucial role in enhancing neuroplasticity by modulating levels of brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF).

Understanding BDNF: BDNF is a neurotrophin essential to the development, maintenance, and plasticity of neurons. It supports learning, memory, and emotional regulation. Estrogen is known to positively regulate BDNF expression. Therefore, its decline during menopause may contribute to the cognitive and mood changes observed in many women. (Lommatzsch et al., 2005)

Exercise-Induced Modulation of BDNF: Resistance training has been shown to significantly increase circulating BDNF levels. For example, a clinical study in postmenopausal women found that regular physical activity elevated BDNF concentrations and improved mood and body composition. (Archer et al., 2020)

Mechanisms: Exercise stimulates key signaling pathways, e.g., PI3K-Akt and MAPK, that upregulate BDNF and promote neuronal health. Additionally, skeletal muscle acts as an endocrine organ by releasing myokines during resistance training, contributing to central nervous system benefits. Finally, exercise reduces systemic inflammation, which otherwise suppresses neurotrophic signaling.

Clinical Implications: Elevating BDNF through training can help protect against menopause-associated cognitive decline and mood instability. Resistance exercise is not merely protective of the body—it actively shapes the brain.

  • Train 3–4 days per week using compound lifts, e.g., squats, deadlifts, presses, rows
  • Apply progressive overload to drive adaptation
  • Incorporate impact-based exercises, e.g., step-ups, jump squats, if joint health allows

3. Targeted Supplementation

While not a replacement for training or diet, supplements can support hormonal and metabolic balance.

  • Creatine: Supports muscle retention, cognition, and mood
  • Magnesium glycinate or threonate: Improves sleep, stress resilience, and neuromuscular function
  • Ashwagandha: Helps regulate cortisol
  • Omega-3s (EPA, DHA): Cardioprotective and anti-inflammatory

Doses: 3–5 g creatine daily, 250–500 mg EPA/DHA per day

4. Cognitive and Emotional Resilience

Hormonal changes affect neurotransmitters, e.g., serotonin and GABA. Symptoms such as anxiety, brain fog, and sleep disruption are real and manageable.

  • Maintain a consistent sleep-wake rhythm
  • Engage in breathwork or journaling for 3–5 minutes daily
  • Prioritize daily outdoor walking for dopamine regulation

5. Hormone Therapy

Bioidentical hormone therapy can alleviate symptoms and protect long-term health when used appropriately. The first decade after menopause is the optimal therapeutic window.

  • Consult a provider trained in integrative or functional endocrinology
  • Ensure personalized evaluation before starting therapy

6. Track to Empower

Feedback systems help translate effort into insight and motivation.

  • Use wearables, e.g., Oura, Garmin, Whoop, to track recovery and sleep
  • Log weekly metrics: mood, strength, energy, sleep quality
  • Focus on trends, not perfection

Closing Thoughts

Menopause is not the end of vitality—it is a strategic inflection point. With the right inputs and systems, this chapter can become one of clarity, strength, and metabolic resilience. Science is the map. Strategy is the method.
